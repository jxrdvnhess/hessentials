Once a day, I sit for five minutes in silence with nothing in my hands and nothing in my ears. No app. No timer voice telling me to "notice my breath." No bell at the start, no bell at the end, no narrator with a soft Australian accent suggesting that I picture a beach. Five minutes. A chair. A wall.

This is the practice. It is brutally simple. Almost nobody does it.

The wellness market has spent the last decade convincing people that meditation is a thing you need an app to learn how to do. The result is that millions of people now do an excellent job of *meditating about meditation* — opening the app, browsing the library, picking a session, tracking their streak — and never get to the practice underneath. The practice underneath is sitting quietly without anyone telling you what to do. The app is a meditation about a meditation. That isn't a trick. That's a market problem.

---

## What's wrong with the app version

The app version isn't bad. It's a fine on-ramp. The problem is that the on-ramp has been mistaken for the road. People who have been "meditating" for two years on an app, daily, are usually not meditating. They are listening to a guided audio track that loosely resembles meditation. The two are different and the difference matters.

A guided meditation does most of the cognitive work for you. It tells you what to attend to, when to notice your wandering, how to bring yourself back, when the session is over. The work of *managing your own attention*, which is the actual practice, has been outsourced. After two years of outsourcing, you have developed the skill of following a meditation track. You have not developed the skill of meditating.

This is roughly the difference between watching cooking videos and learning to cook. Watching is fine. Watching is even instructive at the start. But you do not get better at cooking by watching more videos. You get better at cooking by cooking, badly, in your own kitchen, without anyone telling you what to do.

The same is true here. The actual practice is sitting in silence and managing your own attention. Five minutes is the right starting unit. You will be terrible at it for a while. That's the practice.

---

## What five minutes of unsupported silence actually does

Three things, all of them small enough to miss.

It calibrates the day. After about ninety seconds of sitting quietly, the brain's running level of background noise becomes audible. You notice that you're tracking eight things you didn't realize you were tracking. The noticing itself dampens the tracking. After three minutes, the brain has settled into a slightly lower-frequency state. After five, you have a small reset that the rest of the day is built on top of.

It rebuilds the muscle of unsupported attention. The phone, the podcast, the playlist, and the Slack panel have between them stripped most adults of the experience of holding their own attention without external help. This is not a moral problem; it's a skill problem. Five minutes of supportless silence, every day, slowly retrains the muscle. After about three weeks, you'll notice you can read for longer without picking up the phone. After three months, you'll notice the phone has lost some of its grip — not because you decided it should, but because the substrate underneath wanting it has thinned out.

It makes the day legible to itself. You will, almost without noticing, start picking up signals you'd been missing — the body's actual hunger, the mood that was forming under the surface of the morning, the conversation from yesterday that needs a follow-up. The signals were always there. The five minutes is what gives them room to surface.

None of these effects are dramatic. They compound.

## How to actually do this

Find a chair. Not the floor, not a cushion, not a posture. The floor and the cushion are part of the cosplay version of meditation; they aren't necessary, and the period of sorting out your knees and lower back will compete with the practice. Sit in a normal chair, both feet on the floor, hands in your lap, back upright but not rigid. The body's job during the practice is to be unobtrusive.

Five minutes. Set a timer with a soft sound at the end. The timer is the only piece of equipment. (Yes, this is technically an app. The exception is narrow: a single timer, not a guided track, not an ambient soundscape, not a streak counter.) The point of the timer is that you can stop watching the clock; the point of *not* using a guided track is that the cognitive work is yours.

Eyes closed or eyes resting on a fixed point. Either works. Fixed-point gaze is sometimes easier in early practice because the room remains a stabilizer. Closed eyes are sometimes easier later because the visual field disappears. Pick one. Don't switch back and forth in the middle of the session.

Don't manage your breath. Just notice it. The breath is going to keep happening whether you watch it or not. Watching it is fine. Trying to slow it, deepen it, square-breathe it, or run any other technique on it is the cognitive work the app would be doing for you, and you are trying to *not have it done for you*.

When your mind wanders — and it will, probably continuously — bring it back. That is the entire technique. There is no graduate-level move. The graduate-level move is "do this with less drama" — meaning the moment you notice the wandering, you don't beat yourself up, you don't congratulate yourself, you just come back. The not-making-it-a-thing is the discipline.

Sit through the unpleasantness. You will, in some sessions, be bored. In other sessions, anxious. In other sessions, restless to the point that five minutes feels like fifteen. None of this is the practice failing; this is the practice working. The unpleasantness is information about the substrate the rest of the day is sitting on. Sit with it. The timer will go off. You'll be okay.

When the timer goes off, do not immediately reach for your phone. Sit for another thirty seconds. The thirty seconds at the end is part of the practice — it is where the calibration locks in. If you reach for the phone the moment the timer ends, you've spent five minutes settling and three seconds unraveling it.

## When to do it

Once a day. Same time each day if possible — the consistency matters more than the time of day. Most people I know who do this reliably do it in the morning, before the day's first input. Some do it before lunch, as a midday reset. A small number do it before bed, which is fine but tends to slide toward sleeping rather than sitting.

Five minutes is the right starting dose. Don't try to ramp to twenty. The five-minute version, done daily for a year, beats the twenty-minute version done sporadically for a year, by a wide margin. After a year of consistent five-minute sessions, you can lengthen if it serves the practice. Most people don't need to.

---

## What to ignore

The streak. Streaks gamify the practice and turn it back into something you're doing for an external reward. The point is the practice, not the count. If you miss a day, you miss a day. Tomorrow's session is the only one that matters.

The "right" technique. There is no right technique for unsupported silence. Once you stop running someone else's voice through your head, what's left is *your* attention, doing its work. The technique is whatever brings you back when you wander.

The metrics. Don't track your sessions. Don't journal them. Don't grade them. The instinct to optimize the practice is the rationalizing brain trying to colonize the one part of the day it can't have. Refuse it.

The promise of "calm." Some sessions will end with calm. Others won't. Sessions are not graded by their feel. A session that ended in restlessness still trained the muscle.

## What I actually do

Five minutes, most mornings, in the chair by the kitchen window. Phone face-down, two rooms away. A simple kitchen timer set on the counter — the wind-up kind, because it doesn't run on battery and doesn't pretend to be intelligent. Eyes closed. The street outside is quiet at that hour, but if it weren't, the street would be fine.

I have been doing this in roughly this form for several years. I am not better at meditating than I was a year in. I am better at the rest of the day than I was before I started.

That trade is worth five minutes.

---

The app is not the problem. The app is just a doorway that's been mistaken for a room. The room — five minutes of unsupported silence, daily — is small and unimpressive and free. The room is the practice.

Five minutes. A chair. A wall. That's all.
