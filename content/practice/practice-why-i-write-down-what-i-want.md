There's a piece of paper in my wallet. It's been folded so many times the creases are soft. On it, in pencil — pencil, because the list changes — are five things I'm working toward this year. Not goals. Not affirmations. Wants.

I read them in the morning when I take my keys out of the bowl. I read them at night when I put my keys back. That's the entire practice.

People want to call this manifestation. It isn't. Manifestation is the idea that wanting a thing causes the universe to deliver it. That's not what's happening here. What's happening is that I have a brain that, left to its own devices, will spend Tuesday answering email and Wednesday reorganizing the spice drawer and Sunday wondering where the year went. The list is not magic. The list is a corrective.

---

## What people get wrong about this

The mistake is treating it as visualization. Close your eyes, picture the house, feel the success in your body. That doesn't work, and it doesn't work for a reason — your brain can tell the difference between a fantasy and a target. Fantasies are something the brain does *for* you. Targets are something the brain does *against* you, in the useful sense — they create a low-grade tension between where you are and where you said you wanted to be.

A vague want produces nothing. "I want to be in better shape" is a fantasy. "I want to walk an hour every morning before I open my laptop" is a target. One of those will sit in your head and dissipate. The other will start arguing with you at 7:14 a.m. when you reach for the laptop.

The point isn't to feel good about the want. The point is to make the want specific enough that not pursuing it becomes uncomfortable.

---

## What actually works

Five things, written down. Five is the cap. You won't hold more, and if you can hold more it means most of them aren't real wants — they're hopes you've cataloged in case anyone asks.

Each one stated as a behavior, not a state. "Sober a year" is a state. "Don't drink tonight" is a behavior — and it's the only thing the next twenty-four hours are actually capable of. States are made out of behaviors, repeated, but the unit you control is the behavior.

Pencil, not pen. You will be wrong about what you want. Twice a year I cross something off, not because I achieved it, but because I figured out it wasn't mine — it was someone else's want I'd absorbed and forgotten to question. Pen makes that feel like failure. Pencil makes it feel like editing.

A piece of paper, not a phone note. The phone is the place where wants go to die. You open the note, the lock screen interrupts, you see a text, you answer the text, and the want gets one more day of being unread. Paper is in your hand the moment you reach for your wallet. There's no other software running.

Read it in the same two moments every day. Morning, when the day is theoretically still negotiable. Night, when the day's evidence is in. The morning read is a setup; the evening read is an audit. You don't need to do anything about either one. You just need to see the list.

---

## What this is actually doing

It is shortening the gap between intention and attention.

Most lives don't fail because of a lack of want. They drift because the want got filed somewhere the rest of the day couldn't see it. By Tuesday afternoon, "I want to write a book" is no longer in active memory — what's in active memory is the email subject line you're three sentences into. The list pulls the want back into the room.

There is a kind of person who finds this practice silly because they think they should be able to "just remember." That kind of person is also the kind of person whose calendar is in their head, whose grocery list is in their head, and whose Tuesday afternoon is a little more chaotic than they admit. Externalizing memory is not weakness. It's the only thing the human mind does well at scale, and it's the entire premise of writing as a technology.

The list works because it is *outside you*. It can't be edited by mood. It can't be quietly downgraded by a hard week. It says the same thing in March that it said in January, and in June it forces a decision: either it's still true and you've drifted, or it's no longer true and you should cross it off.

Both outcomes are useful. Drift is information. Editing is information. The list is the instrument that produces the information.

---

## What I actually carry

A folded sheet of unlined paper, roughly the size of a credit card after folding. Written in 0.5 mm pencil so the lines stay readable through fading. Updated twice a year — once at the year's end, once around my birthday. Re-copied any time the paper softens past usefulness. The act of re-copying is part of the practice; if a want survives the re-copy, it's still real. If it doesn't, the hand will hesitate at it, and the hesitation is the answer.

Nothing on the paper has ever been ambitious in a way I'd be embarrassed to read in public. Nothing on it has ever been a metric — no income figures, no follower counts, no weights. Wants on this list are about the shape of the days, not the size of the outcomes. Outcomes follow shape. Shape doesn't follow outcomes.

---

The first time I wrote a list like this, I spent twenty minutes on it and three of the five wants were borrowed. By the next quarter, all five were mine. That edit is the practice. The carrying is just what makes the edit unavoidable.

You don't have to believe in any of it. You just have to write it down and keep it on you. The brain takes it from there.
