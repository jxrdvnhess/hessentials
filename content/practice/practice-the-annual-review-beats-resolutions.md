Sometime between Christmas and the second week of January, most people will write a list of New Year's resolutions and never look at it again. The list will read like the manifesto of a person they have not yet met — fitter, more present, less on their phone, more disciplined with money, kinder, in love, abroad. By February the list is in a drawer. By March they've forgotten what was on it. By June they don't remember writing it.

The resolution is a forecast. Forecasts about humans are hard, and forecasts you make about *yourself* are uniquely hard, because the maker of the forecast and the subject of the forecast are the same person, and the maker is, in late December, full of champagne and goodwill and the particular self-pity of having eaten too much pie. The forecast is unreliable for structural reasons, not for moral ones.

There is a better practice. It runs at roughly the same time of year and it costs roughly the same amount of paper. It is called the annual review. The difference is that a review looks *backward*, at evidence you actually have, instead of forward, at a person who doesn't exist yet.

I do this every year, and the years I have done it well I have improved more than the years I haven't. The mechanism is not magic. The mechanism is that you cannot fix what you can't see, and the review is the only annual instrument that forces you to actually see.

---

## Why resolutions fail

They fail because they're predictions in a forecasting environment that punishes prediction.

A resolution implicitly assumes you know which behavior, if changed, would most improve your life. The version of you making the resolution is using the data available *at the moment of making*, which is dominated by whatever happened in the last three weeks. Three weeks of holiday eating produces a fitness resolution. A bad work November produces a "find a new job" resolution. The resolution is a recency bias dressed up as a plan.

They fail because they're singular. Most people pick one or two big goals — quit drinking, run a marathon, save twenty thousand. Singular goals are brittle: any one missed week, and the goal is "broken," at which point the rationalizing brain offers an exit ramp and you take it. The structure has no graceful failure mode.

They fail because they're external. The resolution lists a behavior. The behavior is the *output* of an underlying system you haven't examined. You can't quit drinking by resolving to quit drinking; you quit drinking by figuring out what the drinking is doing for you and replacing the function. The resolution skips the diagnostic step entirely.

The annual review fixes all three of these.

---

## What the annual review is

It is a structured backward-looking interview with yourself, conducted across a few days in December or January, in which you answer specific questions about the previous year using only evidence — what actually happened, in what order, and how you felt about it.

That's it. The structure produces the value. There is no required template, but the questions cluster:

What did I spend the year doing, in actual hours, that I did not plan to spend it doing?

Which decisions made by me, in this year, am I still grateful for? Which am I quietly trying to talk myself into still being grateful for?

What did I say I'd do this year and not do? Which of those, on inspection, was a real want, and which was a borrowed one?

Where did I show up to my own life, and where did I phone in?

Who got the best of me this year? Who got the leftover version? Was the allocation correct?

What did I learn, by which I mean: what do I now understand that I didn't on January 1?

What was the best meal of the year? The best night? The best book? The hardest week? Why?

What was the year actually about?

Some of these are easy. Most are not. The hard ones are the ones that produce the value.

---

## Why this works

It produces evidence the rationalizing brain can't argue with.

The rationalizing brain is excellent at editing the past in real time. By December, the brain has already produced a tidy narrative of the year that's largely self-serving and generally inaccurate. The narrative says you were busier than you actually were, more disciplined than you actually were, kinder than you actually were, more thoughtful than you actually were. The narrative is comfortable.

The review interrogates the narrative. When you sit down with the calendar, the bank statements, the camera roll, and the running list of what you read and watched, the narrative starts to leak. You realize you spent forty-three Saturday afternoons watching sports, not the eight you'd estimated. You realize the relationship you've been telling yourself is "a slow build" has had three meaningful encounters in eight months. You realize the new project you've been talking about all year exists only as a folder of unread tabs.

This is not a self-flagellation exercise. The point is not to rough yourself up. The point is to update the model of you with the evidence the year produced. An updated model is the only model worth planning from. The next year's plan, if you plan one at all, can now be made by someone who actually knows what the previous year looked like.

The other thing the review produces is a clean read on what *worked*. Most people are surprisingly bad at noticing what's working in their own life — successful patterns become invisible by virtue of being normal. The review surfaces them. Some of last year's quiet rituals — the Sunday morning walks, the Wednesday dinners with one specific friend, the discipline of writing in the morning — turn out to have done a quiet majority of the year's good work. The review tells you to keep them. Resolutions never tell you what to keep.

## How to actually do this

Set aside three uninterrupted hours, in two sessions across two days. The first session is for gathering. The second is for sitting with what you gathered.

For the first session, pull the actual evidence:

The calendar. Scroll through every week, slowly. Photograph or write down the things you'd otherwise forget. Notice the patterns of the year — what kinds of weeks repeated, what didn't.

The camera roll, if you keep one. The phone is a forensic instrument here. Scroll month by month. Notice what's missing — the trip you meant to take and didn't, the people you saw less of than you remembered.

A loose financial summary. Not a budget audit; a quick read on what you actually spent money on, in broad categories. Money is one of the cleanest readings of what a year was actually about, because the spending is harder to lie about than the memory is.

A list of every meaningful piece of media you consumed — books, films, talks, conversations that landed. Not for performance. For evidence of where your attention was.

For the second session, with the gathered evidence in front of you, work through a set of questions like the ones above. Pencil. Don't try to be elegant. Don't try to draw conclusions until you've answered the questions evidentially. Conclusions can come later. The first job is to look honestly.

The output should not be a plan. The output should be a small set of *observations* about the year. Three to seven observations is right. "The year was about restoring a friendship I'd let lapse," might be one. "The year was about discovering that the work I thought I wanted is the work that drains me," might be another. "The year was about the slow understanding that my house, though beautiful, is in the wrong neighborhood," might be a third.

Observations are not actions. That's why they work. An observation is a true sentence about the past. An action is a forecast about the future. The review traffics in the former, on the principle that good actions follow from accurate observations and bad ones follow from inaccurate ones.

If, after sitting with the observations for a week, a clear next-step action presents itself, take it. If not, sit with the observations longer. The actions will emerge in time. Forced actions, drawn prematurely from the review, are just resolutions in a more sophisticated jacket.

## What I actually do

The week between Christmas and New Year's, I block three hours over two days. I open the calendar, the camera roll, a one-line-a-day journal I keep, and a folder where I clip notes and quotes throughout the year. I write down, in pencil, what the year was actually about — usually three to five sentences. I write down what I'd take with me into the next year, and what I'd leave behind. I do not write resolutions. I never have.

The next year tends to organize itself around the observations within a few weeks of natural settling. The actions follow. They are slower and quieter than resolutions, and they are, by an order of magnitude, more durable.

I have done this most years for over a decade. The years I skipped, I noticed for the next two. The years I did it well, the next year went better. The mechanism is not magic. The mechanism is seeing clearly, once a year, what the year was actually about.

---

A resolution is a forecast about a stranger. An annual review is a conversation with the person you actually were.

The conversation is more useful. It is also harder, slower, and lower-status. Most people will keep doing the resolution. The few who do the review will, year over year, gain ground that doesn't show up in any quarterly readout — the ground of a life that is, slowly, being built around what's actually true about the person living it.

Three hours, once a year. Pencil. The calendar. The camera roll. The honest version of the question: *what was the year actually about?*

That's the practice.
